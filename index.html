The Life of C. S. Lewis

  • 1898 — Born in Belfast, Northern Ireland

  • 1917 — Enlisted in the British army

  • 1925 — Elected a Fellow of Magdalen College, Oxford

  • 1931 — Converted to Christianity following a conversation with J.R.R. Tolkien and Hugo Dyson

  • 1933 — Lewis' circle of friends known as The Inklings began meeting together regularly. Members included J.R.R. Tolkien, Warnie, Hugo Dyson, Charles Williams, Dr. Robert Havard, Owen Barfield, Weville Coghill and others.

  • 1935 — Agreed to write the volume on 16th Century English Literature for the Oxford History of English Literature series. Published in 1954, it became a classic.

  • 1937 — Received the Gollancz Memorial Prize for Literature in recognition of The Allegory of Love

  • 1938 — Published The Out of the Silent Planet, the first book in his “Space Trilogy”

  • 1940 — Published The Problem with Pain

  • 1941 — Released The Screwtape Letters in weekly installments

  • 1942 — Gave five live BBC radio talks on the subject “What Christians Believe” in January and February and then a second series of eight talks, entitled “Christian Behavior” in September through November

  • 1944 — Gave seven prerecorded radio talks on the BBC entitled “Beyond Personality”

  • 1947 — Published Miracles. A revised version was republished in 1960

  • 1950 — Published the first book in the Chronicles of Narnia series, The Lion, the Witch and the Wardrobe. The series is widely regarded as a classic of children's literature.

  • 1952 — Published Mere Christianity, one of the most influencial books on Christian teaching in the last century

  • 1954 — Accepted the Chair of Medieval and Renaissance Literature at Cambridge

  • 1955 — Published Surprised by Joy, a partial autobiography that focuses on his conversion to Christianity

  • 1956 — Received the Carnegie Medal in recognition of The Last Battle, the last book in the Chronicles of Narnia series

  • 1957 — Married Joy Davidman at her bedside in Churchill Hospital

  • 1963 — Died on November 22
